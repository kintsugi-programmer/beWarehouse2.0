Mental Models for AI Design
This handout summarizes key mental models to guide in designing within the AI world.
---
1. Human-in-the-Loop
• Core Idea: AI systems should include human oversight at critical points.
• Why It Matters: Ensures accountability, adaptability, and trustworthiness.
• Example: A medical AI suggests diagnoses, but a doctor makes the final call.

---
2. Occam’s Razor
• Core Idea: Favor simplicity when multiple design paths exist.
• Why It Matters: Simple systems are easier to interpret, maintain, and debug.
• Example: A chatbot that answers FAQs directly instead of over-engineering with complex
reasoning.

---
3. Systems Thinking
• Core Idea: AI is part of a larger ecosystem, not a standalone tool.
• Why It Matters: Helps anticipate ripple effects across users, organizations, and society.
• Example: Designing a recommendation engine while considering its impact on user behavior
and business ethics.

---
4. AI as Electricity
• Core Idea: Treat AI as a foundational technology, like electricity.
• Why It Matters: Encourages thinking beyond immediate replacements to entirely new
innovations.
• Analogy: Early electricity replaced candles with lightbulbs, but later enabled microwaves and
washing machines.
• Example: AI not only automates tasks but creates new possibilities

---
5. Designing for Possibilities (Open-Ended Maps)
• Core Idea: Don’t design with rigid, finite goals—design for exploration.
• Why It Matters: Supports adaptability and discovery of unexpected opportunities.
• Analogy: Instead of a metro map with fixed stops, imagine landing in an open field or ocean
with only a partial map.
• Example: Building an AI toolkit that allows users to experiment and find new applications.

---
6. Second-Order Effects
• Core Idea: Think beyond immediate outcomes to anticipate ripple effects.
• Why It Matters: AI systems often create social, economic, and ethical consequences.
• Example: Think about impact on privacy, ethics, and security. A generative AI tool might make
it easy to create fake content. The design choices — such as watermarking outputs, limiting
misuse, or encouraging transparency — directly influence whether the system discourages
harmful behavior or enables it.
---
Prompt Design
In essence, the shift from prompt engineering to prompt design represents a move from simply
fine‐tuning inputs for efficiency to thoughtfully shaping the entire interaction. While prompt
engineering focuses on the technical tweaks to get a desired output, prompt design is about
crafting the interaction itself—it’s a holistic framework that encourages richer, more creative, and
more meaningful AI interactions. This approach doesn’t just optimize outputs; it shapes the
journey, ensuring that the AI becomes a partner in exploration rather than just a tool for
answers.
---
Key Takeaway
AI design is not just about solving today’s problems—it’s about creating platforms, ecosystems,
and possibilities that shape tomorrow.
